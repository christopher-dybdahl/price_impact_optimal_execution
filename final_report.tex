\section{Data Preparation}\label{sec:data}

The panel is 2019 US equities sampled on a 10-second intraday grid,
restricted to twenty stocks whose tickers begin with the letter A,
giving us about $11.0$ million bin rows over $250$ trading days.
For each stock-day we retain a signed traded flow $q_t$ and a midprice
$P_t$.

Two trailing 20-day daily statistics are needed to make impact
coefficients comparable across stocks and days, an intraday volatility
proxy and an average-daily-volume proxy:
\[
  \sigma_{i,d}^2 = \overline{\mathrm{Var}_t\,r_{i,d,t}},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{ADV}_{i,d} = \overline{\textstyle\sum_t |q_{i,d,t}|},
\]
where the overline denotes a 20-day rolling mean per stock.  These two
scales normalise raw flow into a state we can regress on, and
normalise raw returns into a target with a comparable scale.

We use a ten-window rolling protocol with a two-month gap.  Each
window trains on month $m$, tunes the non-parametric shrinkage
hyperparameter $\gamma$ on month $m+1$, and validates on month $m+2$,
for $m = 1, \dots, 10$.

\section{Impact Model Fitting}\label{sec:fit}

\subsection{OW vs AFS}\label{sec:recursion}

Both models build the same Ornstein--Uhlenbeck recursion on a
linearly aggregated flow state $J_t$:
\[
  J_{t+1} = (1-\beta)\,J_t + \frac{q_t}{\mathrm{ADV}_d},
  \qquad
  \beta = \frac{\ln 2}{H_{\text{bins}}},
\]
where $H_{\text{bins}}$ is the half-life in 10-second bins (so a 60-min
half-life corresponds to $H_{\text{bins}} = 360$).  The two models
differ only in the \emph{shape function} $h$, the map from the
aggregated state $J_t$ to the normalised price-impact state
$\bar I_t = \sigma_d\,h(J_t)$:
\[
  \text{OW:}\;\;\bar I_t = \sigma_d\,J_t
  \qquad\text{(linear)},
  \qquad
  \text{AFS:}\;\;\bar I_t = \sigma_d\,\mathrm{sgn}(J_t)\,|J_t|^{c}
  \qquad\text{(concave, } c \in (0,1)\text{)}.
\]
The AFS model allows us to specify the shape $h$ rather than inheriting the linear
form by default; we restrict to the one-parameter power-law family
$h(x) = |x|^{c}$ because it admits a closed-form optimal execution
strategy, so $c$ is the only free shape parameter we fit empirically.

\paragraph{Optimisation problem and regression target.}
For each $(c, H)$ we want the parameter that maximises out-of-sample
$R^2$ in the impact regression:
\[
  \max_{H,\,c,\,\lambda}\; R^2_{\text{OOS}}\!\bigl(H, c, \lambda\bigr).
\]
We select an explanation horizon
$\tau$ bins ($\tau = 6 \Rightarrow$ 1 min) and  the
regression is therefore
\[
  y_t = \lambda\,x_t + \varepsilon_t,
  \qquad
  x_t = \bar I_t - \bar I_{t-\tau},
  \qquad
  y_t = \frac{P_t - P_{t-\tau}}{P_{t-\tau}},
\]
Given $c$ and $H$,
OLS pins $\hat\lambda$ instantly, so the joint optimisation reduces to
a two-dimensional grid over $(c, H)$.

\subsection{Non-parametric extension with cross-stock shrinkage}\label{sec:np}

The parametric fit forces a specific functional form on the impact
response.  As an extension we relax that assumption and estimate the
impact response $g(x)$ non-parametrically with a binned estimator on
both OW-flavoured and AFS-flavoured $x$, using the same procedure for
the two models.  Per-stock bin means $\hat g_{i,b}$ are shrunk toward
a universal pooled curve $\bar g_b$ through
\[
  g_{i,b}^{\mathrm{reg}}(\gamma)
    \;=\; \frac{S_{y,i,b} + \gamma\,\bar g_b}{n_{i,b}+\gamma},
\]
with $B = 15$ quantile bins, edges computed on the train month pooled
across stocks, and $\gamma$ tuned on the held-out test month by
minimising squared-error MSE over a log-spaced grid scaled to the
median bin count.  The shrinkage formula is a posterior-mean
update treating $\bar g_b$ as a Bayesian prior with effective sample
size $\gamma$.

Figure~\ref{fig:curves} shows the estimated $g(x)$ at one window
(train month 3).  Per-stock curves are grey, the universal pooled
curve is the blue dashed line.  Both panels show the same
qualitative shape: a steep slope through the origin that flattens at
the extremes --- i.e., sub-linear impact.  For OW ($c{=}1$) this is
the curvature the linear kernel $\bar I = \sigma\,J$ ignores
entirely; for AFS ($c{=}0.5$) the sqrt kernel has absorbed part of
the curvature, but the response is still visibly concave because the
grid in Table~\ref{tab:grid} prefers $c \approx 0.8$ rather than
$0.5$.

The deeper point is that the move from parametric to non-parametric
trades a one-parameter functional form ($y = \lambda x$) for fifteen
bin-wise degrees of freedom, regardless of which kernel built $x_t$.
Once that flexibility is granted, both models converge on essentially
the same binned shape, which is why the parametric gap of $4.7$ pp
between OW and AFS shrinks to $1.5$ pp under NP (see
Table~\ref{tab:apples}).  The NP lift is comparable across the two
models, and slightly larger for AFS ($+10.0$ pp) than for OW
($+6.8$ pp) because the parametric AFS at $c = 0.5$ starts further
from the underlying impact curve and so leaves more residual
structure for the binned estimator to recover.

\subsection{$\gamma$ Regularisation}\label{sec:gamma}

In the headline configuration the test-month MSE is essentially flat
in $\gamma$ across six orders of magnitude
(Figure~\ref{fig:gamma_progression}, leftmost panel).  Shrinkage has
nothing material to do, because per-stock estimators are already
precise at the headline sample size (median $\sim 2{,}900$
observations per (stock, bin) cell), and the regularisation gain over
the raw per-stock estimator is only $+0.0001$ in pooled OOS $R^2$.

To verify the estimator behaves correctly when shrinkage actually
does matter, we re-run the same protocol with progressively shorter
training windows, going from one month ($\sim 21$ trading days) down
to five days and then to two days, which decreases per-cell $n$ from
$\sim 2{,}940$ down to $\sim 275$.  As Table~\ref{tab:stress} shows,
the optimal shrinkage strength $\gamma^\star/n$ rises from $\sim 0.14$
at the headline to $\sim 1.1$ at two-day windows for OW (and $\sim 1.6$
for AFS), and the OOS $R^2$ gain of regularised over raw per-stock
estimators grows roughly sixty times for OW (from $+0.0001$ to
$+0.0066$) and ninety times for AFS (from $+0.0001$ to $+0.0097$).
Figure~\ref{fig:gamma_progression} shows the corresponding $\gamma$
tuning curves (median across the ten rolling windows): flat at the
headline, developing a clear minimum at non-trivial $\gamma^\star$
as the window shrinks.  The flatness in the headline configuration is
therefore a consequence of the fact that data is abundant, not a
defect of the estimator.

\section{Backtest Engine}\label{sec:bt}

The engine decomposes the flow from the tape into a
residual market component and an attributed ``our trades'' component:
\[
  q_{\text{agg},t} \;=\; q_{\text{others},t} \;+\; q_{\text{us},t}.
\]
It then applies the Waelbroeck impact map: the unperturbed path drives the OU impact state with the residual flow $q_{\text{others}} = q_{\text{agg}} - q_{\text{us}}$ and
the perturbed path drives it with the full observed flow $q_{\text{agg}}$.  Both price paths share as common baseline the cumulative return $\text{cumret}(t) = (P^{\text{mid}}_t - P^{\text{mid}}_0)/P^{\text{mid}}_0$ from the day-open mid:
\begin{align}
  P_{\text{unpert}}(t) &= P_0\bigl(1 + \text{cumret}(t) + g_{\text{others}}(t)\bigr),
  \label{eq:punpert}\\
  P_{\text{pert}}(t) &= P_0\bigl(1 + \text{cumret}(t) + g_{\text{full}}(t)\bigr),
  \label{eq:ppert}
\end{align}
where the impact contribution is $g(t) = \lambda\,\bar I(t)$ for the OW and AFS baselines (and $g(t) = \lambda(t)\,\bar I(t)$ for a time-dependent extended-OW model).
\\
Crucially, $P_{\text{unpert}}$ is not directly observed: it is the model defined Waelbroeck counterfactual "without us" path.  Net P\&L marks the cumulative position $X_t = \sum_{s\le t} q_{\text{us},s}$ along the perturbed path and gross P\&L marks the same position along the unperturbed path, impact cost is the gap:
\begin{align}
  \text{Net P\&L}     &= \textstyle\sum_t X_{t-1}\,\Delta P_{\text{pert},t},
  \label{eq:netpnl}\\
  \text{Gross P\&L}   &= \textstyle\sum_t X_{t-1}\,\Delta P_{\text{unpert},t},
  \label{eq:grosspnl}\\
  \text{Impact cost}  &= \text{Gross} - \text{Net}.
  \label{eq:impact}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Which impact model in the engine?}
The engine treats the impact model as an explicit modelling choice
because the concavity propagates directly from child trades to the
parent-order scaling through the recursion of \S\ref{sec:recursion}:
$J_{t+1} = (1-\beta)J_t + q_t/\mathrm{ADV}$ and
$\bar I_t = \sigma\,\mathrm{sgn}(J_t)\,|J_t|^{c}$.
For a constant-rate parent of size $Q$ executed over duration $T$,
the OU reaches equilibrium $J^\star \propto Q/(T\beta)$ and the
parent-level impact scales as $|Q/T|^{c}$; at $c = 1$ this gives
linear-in-$Q/V$ impact, at lower $c$ it gives a concave law that
sub-linearly penalises larger parents.  Because $\bar I = h(J)$ is a
nonlinear function of the aggregated state, the engine evaluates
$\bar I(q_{\text{agg}})$ and $\bar I(q_{\text{others}})$ from two
separate runs of the linear OU on $J$ rather than splitting impact
additively, which would be inconsistent for $c \ne 1$.

\paragraph{Optimal strategy.}
The OW closed-form strategy sizes a target position at each bin:
\[
  X^\star_t = \frac{\alpha_t \cdot \mathrm{ADV}}{2\,\lambda\,\sigma},
  \qquad
  q_{\text{us},t} = \kappa\,(X^\star_t - X_{t-1}),
  \qquad
  \kappa = \beta,
\]
where $\alpha_t$ is the synthetic signal and $\kappa = \beta$ is the
mean-reversion rate of the OU.  Positions are capped to
$\pm 0.5\%\,\mathrm{ADV}$ and ramped linearly to zero over the
final 30 minutes of the session.  The AFS strategy shares this same
target-position rule with $\bar I^\star_t = \alpha_t/((1+c)\lambda)$, the model-specific power-law nonlinearity enters through the inversion of the recursion in \S\ref{sec:recursion} that maps the target impact state back to a per-bin trade.

\paragraph{Trade providers.}
The simulator accepts any signed schedule for $q_{\text{us}}$ through a callback interface. The pipeline wires the optimal $q$s for the OW and AFS closed forms. This design lets the same engine benchmark arbitrary execution strategies against the Waelbroeck counterfactual without touching the core simulator.

\paragraph{Daily-reset vs.\ multi-day carry.}
The baseline mode (\texttt{carry='daily'}) resets both impact states $\bar I_{\text{others}}$, $\bar I_{\text{full}}$ and the cumulative position to zero at the open of each day for all stocks. The multi-day mode (\texttt{carry='multi'}) carries all three across sessions within a stock: the next day's impact state starts exactly where the previous day ended, as if trading was continuous, with no overnight decay.  This choice has been made because at the fitted $H^\star\approx 52.5$ minutes the OU decay factor over a 16-hour gap would be $(1-\beta)^{16\text{h}\times 6}\approx 10^{-5}$, which is effectively equivalent to wiping $\bar I$. The carry mode matters because inventory continues to be held and the mark-to-market accounting interacts with that carried position.

\paragraph{TCA decomposition.}
Per (stock, day) the engine reports net P\&L (on $P_{\text{pert}}$),
gross P\&L (on $P_{\text{unpert}}$), self-impact cost (gross $-$ net),
predicted-alpha reward
$\sum_t \alpha_t\, q_{\text{us},t}\, P_{\text{unpert},t}$,
and realised-alpha reward
$\sum_t r^{h}_t\, X_t\, P_{\text{unpert},t}$,
along with turnover and gross notional. Transaction costs are omitted, the execution frictions are defined solely by the Waelbroeck impact gap between the two paths.

\paragraph{Configurations.}
The results section sweeps four configurations: models
(OW vs.\ AFS) and carry modes (daily vs.\ multi) with identical
$H^\star$, $\rho$ and position limits.

\section{Synthetic Alpha and Optimal Trading Strategy}\label{sec:alpha}

The synthetic alpha is the standard course construction, calibrated
to a target correlation $\rho$ with the next 6 bins return and made
unbiased so that $E[r^h \mid \alpha_t] = \alpha_t$:
\[
  \alpha_t = r^h_t + y\,\frac{dW_t}{P_t},
  \qquad
  y = \sqrt{(\rho^{-2}-1)\,\mathrm{Var}(r^h)/E[P^{-2}]\,/h},
\]
with $\rho = 0.05$ and $h = 6$ ten-second bin.  Empirical
$\mathrm{Corr}(\alpha,r^h)$ averaged across stocks comes out within
$\pm 1\%$ of the target $\rho$.
\\
The optimal trading strategy under the OW model is the textbook
closed form: the target position is
\[
  X^\star_t = \frac{\alpha_t\,\mathrm{ADV}}{2\,\lambda\,\sigma},
\]
with the running position adjusting toward the target at speed $\kappa = \beta = \ln 2/H_{\text{bins}}$ and a linear ramp to zero over the final 30 minutes of each session to flatten out before the close.  For the AFS variant we use the same target-position rule with the power-law modification $\bar I^\star_t = \alpha_t/((1+c)\lambda)$ and the trade per bin is recovered as described in \S\ref{sec:recursion}.
\\
The extended OW model with a time-dependent $\lambda(t)$ is the third
suggested enhancement.  The simulator can already accept a per-bin
$\lambda_t$ array, and $g(t) = \lambda_t\,\bar I(t)$ falls out of the
same Waelbroeck construction; what is non-trivial is the closed-form
strategy.  When $\lambda$ varies through the day, the HJB-optimal
trade rule is no longer $\kappa$-constant, and so this is a placeholder
in the current submission rather than a working strategy.

Table~\ref{tab:tca} decomposes the same runs into transaction-cost
components.  Net and gross P\&L are both mark-to-market under the
Waelbroeck simulator, and impact cost is the gap between them, which
is well-defined under the engine's ``with us vs without us''
construction.  The realised-alpha reward
$\sum_t \mathrm{fwd\_ret}_t\,X_t\,P_t$ equals gross P\&L by
construction.  The predicted-alpha reward
$\sum_t \alpha_t\,q_t\,P_t$ is dominated by $\alpha$'s noise component
at $\rho = 0.05$, because only $\rho^2 = 0.25\%$ of
$\mathrm{Var}(\alpha)$ is the signal that correlates with realised
return; predicted alpha is therefore informative across configurations
(differences across rows) but not for absolute level (the per-row
magnitudes are inflated by the noise contribution).  Slippage
components that would require fill-price detail (arrival vs VWAP)

Figures~\ref{fig:owdaily}~and~\ref{fig:owmulti} show the year-long
cumulative net P\&L (blue solid) and gross mid-P\&L (orange dashed)
for the OW model under the two carry modes.

\begin{figure}[H]

\caption{Cumulative net (Waelbroeck) and gross (mid) P\&L for the OW
         model, daily-reset carry: Sharpe $+3.43$, net $\$107$k,
         impact $\$11$k.}
\label{fig:owdaily}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/cum_pnl_ow_multi.png}
\caption{Same, multi-day carry: Sharpe $+2.05$, net $\$467$k.  The
         net path \emph{exceeds} the gross path because the
         Waelbroeck mark-to-market credits the carried long position
         for its own simulated price displacement; we treat this as
         an accounting artefact, discussed below.}
\label{fig:owmulti}
\end{figure}

Three observations.  First, the daily-reset run wins on Sharpe ratio
($+3.43$ versus $+2.05$) with much tighter drawdowns ($-\$14$k versus
$-\$88$k), because resetting at the close stops inventory from
accumulating and so keeps the absolute size of the impact channel
small.  Second, the multi-day run wins on absolute dollars (roughly
four times the net P\&L), because the strategy ends up running with
a non-trivial long inventory through the year-long upward trend.
Third, the multi-day run's net P\&L exceeds its gross P\&L, producing
the reported negative impact cost of $-\$39$k.  This is a real
artefact of the Waelbroeck mark-to-market when long positions persist
across sessions: the simulated price drifts up faster than the
undistorted mid because our own buying contributes to
$\bar I^{\text{sim}}$, and marking the carried inventory to that
elevated path produces more P\&L than marking to the mid.  We are not
generating free money; what we are doing is using a P\&L attribution
rule that was designed for liquidated positions, not for persistent
inventory.  A clean impact-cost decomposition under multi-day carry
would need an arrival-price implementation-shortfall benchmark, which
we did not implement here.  Turnover, by contrast, is essentially
identical between the two carry modes (about \$84M in both cases);
only the cost attribution changes.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis and Stress Testing}\label{sec:sens}

We sweep the four design dimensions that the assignment prompts call
out as natural stress tests, with all rows run as full year-long
backtests under the OW model with daily-reset carry.
Table~\ref{tab:sens} reports the results.

Four points follow from the table.

\textbf{Wrong model is catastrophic.}  Fitting $\hat\lambda$ under OW
and then sizing positions as if impact were AFS produces a Sharpe of
$-2.9$ and a net loss of $-\$1.41$M over the year, with around $\$1.54$M
of impact cost dwarfing the still-positive gross alpha capture.  Model
risk dominates signal risk by a wide margin in this strategy, which is
itself a worthwhile finding: the choice between OW and AFS in the
engine is not a benign theoretical preference.  Figure~\ref{fig:worst}
visualises this: the orange dashed gross path still builds, but the
blue net path collapses early and never recovers.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/cum_pnl_wrong_model.png}
\caption{Anatomy of the worst case.  OW-sized positions traded against
         AFS-flavoured impact: gross alpha capture (orange dashed)
         still builds because the signal is real, but the impact bill
         ($\sim \$1.54$M) collapses the net path early and
         permanently.  Model risk dominates signal risk.}
\label{fig:worst}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Signal delay erases the edge.}  A one-minute (six-bin) delay
of the alpha signal drops Sharpe from $+3.43$ to essentially zero
($-0.02$).  Our synthetic alpha is by construction a one-bin-ahead
forecast, so any delay larger than the horizon obliterates the signal.

\textbf{$\rho$ scales Sharpe roughly linearly.}  At
$\rho \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10\}$ we observe Sharpe ratios of
$\{0.62, 3.43, 7.37\}$, an almost linear relationship.  The strategy
is operating in a regime where impact cost grows slowly with signal
strength and so the alpha capture scales close to linearly.

\textbf{Half-life is forgiving.}  Halving $H$ to 30 min lifts Sharpe
to $+5.22$ and doubling it to 120 min drops Sharpe to $+2.51$, both
within a $[+2.5, +5.2]$ band.  This is consistent with the flatness
of the OOS $R^2$ surface in the same region documented in
\S\ref{sec:halflife}: the strategy is not over-tuned to a single
half-life.

\paragraph{Fitting-side stress.}
The non-parametric $\gamma$ regularisation stress test of
\S\ref{sec:gamma} doubles as a sensitivity check on the fitting side.
When the training window shrinks from one month to two days the
optimal shrinkage strength $\gamma^\star/n$ rises from $\sim 0.14$ to
$\sim 1.1$ for OW (and $\sim 1.6$ for AFS), and the OOS $R^2$ gain over
the raw per-stock estimator grows by two orders of magnitude (from
$\sim 10^{-4}$ to $\sim 10^{-2}$, roughly sixty-fold for OW and
ninety-fold for AFS).  This is the ``what if I had much less data
than I assumed'' scenario, and the estimator's behaviour is
regime-correct.

\section{What We Learned}\label{sec:conc}

\paragraph{The power-law subfamily and the $(c, H)$ grid.}
Fitting the joint $(c, H)$ grid under a no-intercept regression with
$\tau = 6$ bins, the best specification on the twenty-stock panel sits
at $(c^\star, H^\star) = (0.80, 10 \text{ min})$ with median pooled OOS
$R^2 = 0.164$.  The improvement over plain OW ($c = 1$, $H = 60$ gives
$R^2 = 0.152$) is about $1.2$ percentage points on a broad ridge
spanning $c \in [0.7, 0.9]$ and $H \in [10, 60]$, so the data do
prefer a mildly concave specification over the linear OW form.  What
the data also say clearly is that strongly concave specifications are
materially worse than the ridge: $c \le 0.5$ loses roughly $3$ to $6$
percentage points of OOS $R^2$ at every $H$.  For continuity with the
prior presentation, the subsequent NP-extension and backtest sections
adopt $c = 0.5$ for the AFS model rather than the grid-optimal
$c = 0.8$ (see addendum in \S\ref{sec:halflife}).

\paragraph{Enhancement 1: non-parametric impact with $\gamma$ regularisation.}
Under the canonical-outside pipeline at $H^\star = 52.5$ min and the
continuity choice $c = 0.5$, the fifteen-bin estimator lifts pooled
OOS $R^2$ from $0.161$ to $0.230$ for OW ($+6.8$ pp) and from $0.114$
to $0.215$ for AFS ($+10.0$ pp).  The NP estimator almost equalises
the two models because it trades a one-parameter form for fifteen
bin-wise degrees of freedom and the extra flexibility recovers
essentially the same impact curve regardless of which kernel built
$x_t$; the parametric gap of $4.7$ pp between OW and AFS shrinks to
$1.5$ pp under NP.  Cross-stock $\gamma$ regularisation is immaterial
in the data-rich headline configuration ($\gamma^\star/n
\sim 0.14\text{--}0.21$, gain $\sim 10^{-4}$), but $\gamma^\star/n$
rises to $\sim 1.1\text{--}1.6$ and the OOS $R^2$ gain rises by two
orders of magnitude as the training window shrinks tenfold, so the
estimator behaves correctly when shrinkage is actually needed.

\paragraph{Enhancement 2: multi-day carry backtest.}
Carrying $\bar I$ and inventory across sessions quadruples gross P\&L
($\$118$k to $\$428$k in the OW configuration), because accumulated
long inventory rides the upward trend.  Daily-reset wins on Sharpe
($+3.43$ versus $+2.05$) with tight drawdowns; multi-day wins on
absolute dollars but produces a negative reported impact cost
($-\$39$k) as an artefact of the Waelbroeck mark-to-market on persistent
inventory.  This is an accounting effect, not free money, and a clean
impact-cost decomposition for persistent inventory needs a different
benchmark (for instance an arrival-price implementation shortfall),
which is not implemented here.

\paragraph{Honest limitations.}
Two of the suggested enhancements are flagged but not implemented in
this submission.  The overnight-alpha extension to the synthetic alpha
was not built because the multi-day carry enhancement absorbed the
available time.  The extended OW model with time-dependent $\lambda(t)$
has its simulator side worked out (the Waelbroeck construction
generalises straightforwardly to $g(t) = \lambda(t)\,\bar I(t)$) but
the corresponding HJB-optimal trade rule is non-trivial, because it is
no longer $\kappa$-constant when $\lambda$ varies, and we did not
derive it within the project window.
